## Slide 3 — Client Context

**Nasamotor Today:**
- Multi-brand automotive dealership group operating in Portugal
- Core system: DMS (Dealer Management System)
- DMS covers standard transactional processes (vehicle registration, invoicing)
- **DMS does NOT cover:** Several key sales workflows

**The Challenge:**
> Nasamotor's sales team manages critical processes manually — through emails, spreadsheets, and phone calls — because the DMS doesn't support them.

**Result:**
- Errors, delays, and poor visibility into sales pipeline
- No scalability as business grows
- Inconsistent customer experience across locations

---

**What to say:**  
"Let's set the context. Nasamotor's DMS does a good job on transactional tasks — registrations, invoicing, basic records. But it was never designed to manage the full complexity of a modern automotive sales process. Things like lead qualification, multi-step approval workflows, trade-in evaluations, financing coordination — all of these happen outside the system, through email chains and spreadsheets. This creates invisible risk: errors, delays, and a sales process that simply can't scale."

**Option 1 — Standard Application Development**  
> Custom-built web/mobile application using conventional software engineering

**Option 2 — Vibe Coding / Low-Code**  
> Rapid digitalization using Power Apps, Power Automate, or similar low-code platforms

**Option 3 — AI Agentic Approach**  
> AI agents orchestrate and automate the sales process end-to-end

**Each option targets the same processes but with very different:**
- Build approach & timeline
- Cost structure
- Automation level
- Maintenance requirements

---

**What to say:**  
"We evaluated three fundamentally different approaches to solve this problem. Option 1 is traditional software development — build exactly what you need, the way you need it. Option 2 is the low-code / vibe coding route — fast, business-user-friendly, using platforms like Microsoft Power Apps. Option 3 is the frontier — AI agents that can autonomously manage and orchestrate parts of the sales process. Each has real merits, and each has real trade-offs. Let's go through them."

## Slide 7 — Option 1: Standard Application Development

**The Approach:**
- Custom web application built with modern frameworks
- **Frontend:** React or Angular (responsive, mobile-friendly)
- **Backend:** .NET / Node.js / Java REST API
- **Database:** PostgreSQL or Azure SQL
- **DMS Integration:** REST API / database connector

**Covers:**
- Digital workflow engine (configurable steps & transitions)
- Role-based task management
- Document upload, preview & approval flows
- Automated notification engine (email, SMS, push)
- Full audit trail & reporting dashboard
- Admin panel for process configuration

---

**What to say:**  
"Option 1 is the traditional enterprise approach: a custom-built application, designed from the ground up to match Nasamotor's exact processes. We're talking a React frontend for a clean, modern user experience — accessible on any browser or mobile device. A .NET or Node.js backend managing the business logic. A SQL database. And a direct API integration with the DMS so there's zero double data entry. The application would be fully owned by Nasamotor — no platform dependency, no licensing constraints."

---

## Slide 8 — Option 1: Pros & Cons

| ✅ Pros | ❌ Cons |
|---|---|
| Fully customizable to Nasamotor's exact needs | Highest upfront development cost |
| No vendor lock-in — full ownership | Longest time-to-market: 6–12 months |
| Enterprise-grade security & control | Requires dedicated development team |
| Precise DMS integration | Risk of scope creep & over-engineering |
| Scalable by design if architected well | Ongoing maintenance responsibility |
| Foundation for future AI layer (Option 3) | Users need training on custom UI |

---

**What to say:**  
"The strengths of Option 1 are clear: it gives you exactly what you need, with no compromises, and you own it completely. The weaknesses are equally clear: it's expensive to build, it takes time, and you need a team to maintain it. The 6-to-12 month timeline is a real constraint in a fast-moving business context. That said, Option 1 produces the cleanest, most scalable foundation — and it's the one most naturally suited to adding AI capabilities later."

---

## Slide 9 — Option 2: Low-Code / Vibe Coding Approach

**The Approach:**
- Build with low-code / no-code platforms
- **Platform options:** Microsoft Power Apps, OutSystems, Mendix, Bubble
- **Workflow automation:** Power Automate, Make (Integromat), Zapier
- **Data layer:** Microsoft Dataverse, SharePoint Lists, Airtable
- **DMS integration:** Pre-built connectors + custom API connectors

**Covers the same process steps as Option 1 — faster:**
- Forms & multi-step approval workflows
- Automated email/Teams notifications
- Document management & e-signature integration
- Dashboards via Power BI (native integration)
- Mobile apps in hours, not months

---

**What to say:**  
"Option 2 uses low-code platforms — most likely Microsoft Power Apps and Power Automate if Nasamotor is already on Microsoft 365, which appears to be the case. The core idea is: instead of writing code for every button and every workflow, you configure them visually. A business analyst or even a trained power user can build and modify workflows. This dramatically cuts build time — from months to weeks per process. The trade-off is that you're working within the constraints of the platform."

---

## Slide 10 — Option 2: Pros & Cons

| ✅ Pros | ❌ Cons |
|---|---|
| Fastest time-to-market: 4–8 weeks per process | Vendor lock-in (platform dependency) |
| Business users can build and modify | Limited customization for complex logic |
| Lower initial development cost | License costs grow with user count |
| Microsoft ecosystem alignment (M365) | Performance limits at scale |
| Built-in Power BI & Teams integration | Technical debt if poorly governed |
| Easier change management & adoption | Complex DMS integrations require custom dev |

---

**What to say:**  
"Option 2's biggest advantage is speed. In 8 weeks, we could have a working digital workflow for 3 or 4 key processes. That's real value, fast. The risk is that low-code platforms have limits — when Nasamotor's processes are complex or unusual, those limits become walls. And license costs can surprise you: Power Apps licenses start manageable but scale up quickly as more users are added. The key to making Option 2 work is governance — having clear rules about who builds what and how."

---

## Slide 11 — Option 3: AI Agentic Approach

**The Approach:**
- AI agents replace manual coordination and decision-making steps
- **AI backbone:** Azure OpenAI / GPT-4o
- **Orchestration:** LangGraph, Microsoft Copilot Studio, AutoGen
- **Tools:** DMS APIs, document readers, CRM, email, calendar
- **Human-in-the-loop:** For exceptions, approvals, edge cases

**Multi-Agent Sales Process Architecture:**

```
User / Customer Input
        ↓
 [ORCHESTRATOR AGENT]
   ↙      ↓      ↓      ↘
[Agent 1] [Agent 2] [Agent 3] [Agent 4]
 Lead     Quote     Contract  Delivery
Intake    Gen       Check     Coord
        ↓
  Human Review (if needed)
        ↓
  DMS + CRM Update
```

---

**What to say:**  
"Option 3 is the most ambitious approach. Instead of just digitalizing what humans do today, we design AI agents that can perform parts of the sales process autonomously. An orchestrator agent coordinates the full process and delegates to specialized sub-agents. Agent 1 qualifies leads and routes them. Agent 2 generates quotes by reading the vehicle catalog and pricing rules. Agent 3 checks contracts for compliance. Agent 4 coordinates delivery logistics. Humans are only involved for exceptions and final approvals. This is human-in-the-loop AI — not fully autonomous."

---

## Slide 12 — Option 3: Agent Capabilities Deep Dive

**What each AI agent does:**

| Agent | Input | Output | Automation Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lead Intake Agent | Email / form / WhatsApp | Qualified lead record in CRM | 90% automated |
| Quote Generator Agent | Vehicle specs + pricing rules + customer profile | Draft quote + PDF | 80% automated |
| Contract & Compliance Agent | Contract document | Validation report + flagged issues | 85% automated |
| Delivery Coordinator Agent | Confirmed sale + logistics calendar | Delivery schedule + customer notification | 75% automated |
| Orchestrator Agent | All above inputs | End-to-end process state + escalations | 95% automated |

---

**What to say:**  
"Let me give you more detail on what each agent actually does. The Lead Intake Agent reads incoming emails or web forms and creates a structured lead record — it decides if the lead is qualified and routes it to the right salesperson. The Quote Generator reads the vehicle catalog and pricing rules and produces a draft quote — the salesperson reviews and approves before sending. The Contract Agent reads a contract document and checks it against legal and compliance rules. And the Delivery Coordinator handles the logistics scheduling. In all cases, humans review and approve — the AI handles the grunt work."

---

## Slide 13 — Option 3: Pros & Cons

| ✅ Pros | ❌ Cons |
|---|---|
| Highest level of process automation | Highest complexity & implementation risk |
| Reduces manual effort by 60–80% | AI errors can cause business problems |
| Scales without adding headcount | Requires robust data & process map first |
| Learns and adapts over time | Regulatory / compliance review needed |
| Competitive differentiation | Cultural change management intensive |
| Future-ready architecture | Longer ROI timeline (24–36 months) |

---

**What to say:**  
"Option 3's upside is transformational — if it works well, you're looking at 60–80% reduction in manual coordination effort. That means your sales team can focus on what they're best at: selling. The downside is that this is the most complex and highest-risk option. An AI agent that makes a mistake in a contract check or sends the wrong quote could cause real business problems. That's why human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable, and why this option requires a mature data and process foundation to work safely."

---

**What to say:**  
"Let's be honest about maintenance. Option 2 is the easiest to maintain in the short term because the platform vendor handles infrastructure and updates. Option 1 requires a dedicated development resource. Option 3 has the most complex maintenance profile — you're not just maintaining code, you're maintaining AI behavior. Prompts need to be versioned. Model updates can change behavior unexpectedly. You need to build in monitoring and quality validation as a permanent operational process."
